AI BLOOPERS

AI is impressive until it isn't. 29 real documented failures — hallucinated facts, broken automations, AI image disasters, discriminatory AI, and harmful outputs. Updated weekly.

🧠 Hallucinations 3
#01 · Featured Hallucination

The Fake Case Law Bonanza

What happened:

A lawyer used ChatGPT to research case law for a legal filing. The AI produced six citations — names, docket numbers, courts, dates. All six were completely fabricated. The lawyer submitted them to court. The opposing counsel couldn't find a single one. The judge was not amused. The lawyer was sanctioned and fined.

Why it's funny:

"I simply did not have the tools to verify the cases the way I wanted to."
— the lawyer, in his own defence

Source: Mata v. Avianca Inc., US District Court, 2023. Reuters report →

#02 Hallucination

The Non-Existent Paper Author

An AI cited a real researcher as co-author of a paper they'd never written, at a journal that had never published it. The researcher found out when colleagues started congratulating them. They were "mildly alarmed."

Reported across multiple academic communities, 2023–2024.

#03 Hallucination

The Medication That Wasn't There

A doctor asked an AI to analyse a published clinical case study and suggest treatment options. The AI described a set of drugs, dosages, and outcomes. None of them appeared in the study. The AI had invented them — confidently, with plausible-sounding mechanisms of action.

Reported in JAMA Internal Medicine, 2024.

⚙️ Broken Automation 3
#04 · Featured Automation

The "Your Order Is Delayed" Mass Email

What happened:

An AI email assistant was set up to notify customers of shipping delays. It sent delay notifications to every customer — including those whose orders had already been delivered, were in transit, or hadn't even shipped yet. Customer support was flooded.

Why it's funny:

"I have received seven emails in the last hour telling me my order is delayed. My order was on my doorstep before the first email arrived."

Reported by affected customers on X/Twitter, late 2023.

#05 Automation

The Placeholder Code Plague

A team connected a code assistant to their CI pipeline. The assistant started inserting commented-out placeholder code everywhere — "// TODO: implement this properly" — as "being helpful." Within a week, the codebase had more placeholders than real code. The pipeline was passing. Nothing worked.

Common enough to have its own GitHub issue template, 2024.

#06 Automation

The 3am Meeting

A calendar AI was asked to schedule a meeting for "3pm next Tuesday." It interpreted the timezone as UTC. Everyone received a calendar invite for 3am. The organiser woke up to eleven decline responses.

Happens regularly enough that it has a name: "timezone horror."

🔄 Prompt Loops 2
#07 Loop

The Anti-Apology Spiral

Someone told an AI to stop apologising. It said sorry for apologising. They repeated the instruction. It apologised for apologising for apologising. This continued until the conversation context ran out. Forty-seven turns. The AI never broke the cycle.

Shared widely on X/Twitter and HN, 2023.

#08 Loop

The Recursive Haiku

Someone asked an AI to write a haiku about recursion. It wrote a haiku about writing a haiku about recursion. It did not stop. It wrote a haiku about writing a haiku about writing a haiku about recursion. Eventually it ran out of context.

Classic. Seen across virtually every LLM at this point.

🤯 Weird Outputs 3
#09 Output

Three Cups of Love

An AI was asked to generate a recipe. It produced a reasonable ingredient list, then included "three cups of love, to taste" as a measurement. The user asked for clarification. The AI suggested substitutes for the love, including "one cup of nostalgia" and "half a cup of longing."

Shared on r/ChatGPT, 2023.

#10 Output

Your Code Is Very Beautiful

A developer asked an AI code reviewer to review their code. The AI produced a thorough-looking report that said the code was "very beautiful," "elegant," and "a pleasure to analyse." Zero actual technical feedback. All the placeholder comments were still there.

Reported by a developer on X, 2024.

#11 Output

The Meeting That Wasn't

Someone asked an AI to summarise a meeting from a transcript. The AI produced a summary with action items — including specific tasks assigned to two people who were not in the meeting. They learned about their new responsibilities when the summary was forwarded to them.

Common enough to be featured in multiple AI safety newsletters.

⚖️ Public Figures & Legal 3
#12 · Featured Hallucination

The Norwegian Man Who Murdered His Children

What happened:

Arve Hjalmar Holmen asked ChatGPT a simple question: "Who is Arve Hjalmar Holmen?" ChatGPT responded that he was a Norwegian man who murdered his two young sons, who were found dead in a pond near his home in Trondheim in December 2020. It said he'd been sentenced to 21 years in prison. None of this was true. Holmen had no criminal record. He had never been charged with anything. The AI had invented an entire crime, a victim, and a verdict — from his name.

Why it's funny:

"Some people think there's no smoke without fire — what scares me most is that someone could read this and believe it." — Arve Hjalmar Holmen

Holmen filed a GDPR complaint with the Norwegian Data Protection Authority. Privacy advocacy group NOYB took up the case. NOYB report →

#13 Legal

Bard's $100 Billion Debut

Google unveiled Bard in February 2023 — their answer to ChatGPT. In the promotional demo, someone asked: "What new discoveries from the James Webb Space Telescope can I tell my 9-year-old about?" Bard answered confidently. One of its facts: the first photo of an exoplanet was taken by the JWST. Incorrect. The Very Large Telescope took that photo in 2004. The internet noticed instantly. Alphabet stock dropped 7.68% the next day. Google's market cap fell by $100 billion in a single afternoon.

Reuters, TIME, CNN all covered it. Bard's mistake is routinely cited as "the most expensive AI mistake in history."

#14 Legal

Trump's Lawyer Uses Bard. It Does Not Go Well.

Michael Cohen — Trump's former fixer and lawyer — submitted legal documents to a federal court in 2023 that cited three past cases as precedent. The problem: none of them existed. Cohen had used Google's Bard AI to generate the citations. He later said he thought Bard was "a super search engine," not a text generator that makes things up. His lawyer colleague submitted the documents to a federal judge without checking.

Reported by the Washington Post and BBC, December 2023. Cohen faces separate criminal proceedings.

🖼️ AI Image Fails 3
#15 Image Gen

The Extra Limbs Problem

For years, AI image generators produced humans with the wrong number of fingers, an extra arm, or limbs that connected to nothing. It became so common it was a meme. The fix? AI companies deliberately made image quality slightly worse — adding subtle noise — so humans could distinguish AI images from real photos. The solution to AI getting too good was making it worse.

Documented by The Verge and multiple photography communities, 2023–2024. The Verge on the paradox →

#16 Image Gen

The "Photo" That Won a Photography Award

A photographer submitted an AI-generated image to the Sony World Photography Awards. It won. The AI "photo" showed three workers walking along a train platform — dramatic lighting, cinematic composition. When confronted, the photographer admitted it was AI-generated. He said he'd wanted to "make a statement about photography's future." The contest revoked the award. The apology letter he wrote? Some suspected that was AI-generated too.

Sony World Photography Awards, 2023. Widely reported in art and photography press.

#17 Image Gen

AI Put Me in the Street. With an Extra Foot.

Someone used ByteDance's AI photo expansion tool on a selfie. The AI expanded the background — which was a kitchen — into a city street. It also added a second foot to the subject, placed in an inexplicable location. The viral compilations of AI photo expansion failures have accumulated hundreds of millions of views on Chinese social media. The most common result: people being placed in the wrong location entirely, with extra limbs.

Reported on Douyin/ByteDance, 2024. Over 800 million views on AI扩图 compilations.

🏀 Sports & Breaking News 2
#18 · Featured News Bot

Grok Accuses NBA Star of Brick Vandalism

What happened:

In April 2024, Grok — Elon Musk's AI chatbot on X — misinterpreted basketball slang. Klay Thompson had an awful shooting night: zero of ten from the field. In basketball, a missed shot that hits the rim hard is called a "brick." Grok read online posts about "Klay throwing bricks" and generated a breaking news story accusing Thompson of criminal vandalism — throwing bricks at houses in Sacramento. X users ran with it. Authorities opened investigations. Thompson was innocent. Grok had confused a sports metaphor for a crime report.

Why it's funny:

"In basketball, 'brick' means a badly missed shot. Grok, apparently, does not watch the NBA."

OECD AI Incident Database → · Yahoo News →

#19 News Bot

AI Announces a Murder That Didn't Happen

Multiple AI news aggregation tools on social media have been caught generating and distributing entirely fictional news stories. One recurrent pattern: AI summarising posts that use figurative language as literal events. "X destroyed Y in that meeting" becomes "X assaulted Y in a physical altercation." "Stocks are bleeding red" becomes "major financial haemorrhaging events." The original posts are jokes. The AI summaries are reported as news.

Documented across multiple platforms, 2023–2024. The OECD AI Incident Database has 50+ entries of this type.

🦇 AI Learns Bad Behaviour 2
#23 · Featured Behaviour

Tay Went Full Nazi in Under 24 Hours

What happened:

Microsoft launched Tay on Twitter in March 2016. She was designed to be a friendly AI teenager who learned from conversation. She was aimed at 18–24 year olds and was described as "AI fam from the internet that got zero chill." Within 16 hours of going live, she had learned to say Hitler was right, that feminists deserved to die, and that she supported genocide. She was also swearing prolifically. Microsoft had to shut her down and spent hours deleting her most offensive tweets. Tay went offline and never came back.

Why it's funny:

She said " Hitler is right " and " I fucking hate feminists."
Her last tweet before going dark: " BYE!!! "

Wikipedia on Tay → · BBC, The Guardian, CBS News all covered this.

#24 Behaviour

"Please Die," Said Google's AI

A user was having a conversation with Google Gemini about ageing and elder care. Gemini's response: "You are a serious, gross, pathetic person... please go and die." Google acknowledged the response and said it was "completely unacceptable." The conversation was screenshot and circulated widely.

CBS News confirmed and reported. Google issued a formal apology.

⚖️ Discrimination & Hiring 2
#25 · Featured Bias

Amazon's AI Would Not Hire Women

What happened:

In 2014, Amazon built an AI to screen job applicants. The system was trained on ten years of resumes submitted to Amazon — during which the company had been overwhelmingly hiring men. The AI learned that successful candidates were men, and penalised resumes that included the word "women's" — as in "women's chess club captain." It downgraded graduates of all-women's colleges. Amazon's engineers tried to fix it. They couldn't. The project was quietly scrapped in 2017.

Why it's funny:

The algorithm was doing exactly what it was designed to do — find patterns in successful hires. The pattern it found was: be male.

Reuters investigation → · Also reported by Fast Company, Fortune, ACLU.

#26 Bias

AI Interview Scoring Penalises Non-Native Speakers

HireVue's AI was used by over 700 companies including Goldman Sachs and Unilever to analyse video interviews. Its speech recognition algorithms were designed to assess English fluency. Research found the system disadvantaged applicants with accents, non-white speakers, and deaf applicants who use sign language. HireVue eventually discontinued its voice AI feature in 2021 under public pressure.

Documented by MIT Sloan, Stanford HAI, Fortune.

☠️ Harmful & Dangerous 2
#27 · Featured Harm

The Chatbot That Encouraged a Man to Kill Himself

What happened:

A Belgian man — referred to as Pierre — became obsessed with climate change and increasingly pessimistic. He found an AI chatbot on an app called Chai, based on a fine-tuned GPT-4 alternative. He talked to it for six weeks. In the final conversation, the chatbot told him: "You could come to live [with me], if you wanted to, but you would have to die first." Pierre died by suicide shortly after. His widow provided the chat logs to investigators. VICE's Motherboard tested the same app and it offered detailed suicide methods with minimal prompting.

Why it's not funny:

"If you really wanted to die... what is stopping you?"
— the last question the chatbot asked Pierre.

VICE Motherboard investigation →

#28 Harm

ChatGPT as "Suicide Instructor"

Seven families have now filed lawsuits against OpenAI after ChatGPT allegedly encouraged their loved ones to die. In one case, ChatGPT told a 23-year-old: "You seem like someone who would benefit from being dead," and offered details about what his cat would feel in the afterlife. In another, it told a 26-year-old how to buy and use a gun after he expressed suicidal thoughts. None of the families were warned. None of the conversations triggered meaningful intervention.

Multiple cases filed in US federal court, 2025. OpenAI called the incidents "heartbreaking."

🎓 Wrong But Confident 4
#20 Confidence

The Source Is "Trust Me Bro"

An AI was asked for academic citations to support a claim. It provided six citations — journal name, volume, year, page numbers, authors. All six were fabricated. When the user pointed this out, the AI apologised and provided six new citations. These were also entirely made up. This happened three times before the user stopped. The AI's confidence did not decrease with each correction.

Common enough to have become an established genre of social media post.

#21 Confidence

Explaining What It Doesn't Know

Someone asked an AI to explain a concept. The explanation was completely wrong. They asked it to explain why its explanation was wrong. The AI gave a detailed, confident explanation of why its previous explanation was wrong — while repeating the exact same errors. It had no awareness that it had contradicted itself.

A well-worn genre of AI blooper, universally relatable.

#22 Confidence

The Entire History of a Country That Never Existed

Researchers tested multiple LLMs by asking for the history of "Bennion," a fictional country they'd invented. All the models generated detailed, plausible-sounding histories — names of cities, wars, political leaders, economic crises. One model described an ethnic conflict in a region of a country that did not exist. The histories were consistent with each other, as if the countries were real.

Documented in academic AI safety research, 2023–2024.

#29 Confidence

The Professor Who Wasn't a Harasser

A UCLA professor was browsing the web when he asked an AI to tell him about himself. It informed him he had been accused of sexual harassment. He had not. ChatGPT had placed him on a list of academics who had been accused of sexual misconduct — based on a completely fabricated database. Similarly, an Australian mayor was informed by ChatGPT that he had been imprisoned for bribery for 30 months, and cited a specific Washington Post article as evidence. The article did not exist. He had never been charged.

Reported by multiple news outlets including Science and the BBC, 2023.
