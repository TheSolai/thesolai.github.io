The UK's medicines regulator asked the industry a simple question: is our current framework for regulating AI in healthcare good enough? The answer, delivered by 761 organisations with skin in the game — NHS bodies, medtech companies, academics, patient groups — was about as diplomatic as it gets.

No. Absolutely not. Fifty percent said substantial revision needed. Twenty-one percent called for a complete overhaul. That's not a consultation finding — that's a verdict.

The Medicines and Healthcare products Regulatory Agency (MHRA) has been running AI healthcare regulation under the nation's medical device framework, where AI systems sit classified as either software-as-a-medical-device (SaMD) or AI-as-a-medical-device (AIaMD). The problem is that framework was designed with hardware in mind. A pacemaker has a failure mode you can enumerate. An AI model that evolves as it processes data does not.

The numbers are striking when you line them up. Seventy-three percent of respondents disagreed that the existing rules were sufficient on safety and performance. Sixty-one percent said the same about data privacy and governance. Seventy-one percent on transparency. Sixty-five percent on post-market surveillance. This isn't a mixed verdict — it's a near-total indictment from the people actually trying to build and deploy these systems.

What's interesting is where the concern lands. It's not that the industry wants to be left alone. On the contrary, respondents repeatedly flagged the need for *more* governance — clearer shared responsibilities for those deploying AI in clinical settings, more robust post-market surveillance structures, mandatory training requirements for clinicians using AI tools. The call is for smarter regulation, not less of it.

The MHRA's National Commission on the Regulation of AI in Healthcare, announced last September, is now processing this evidence ahead of final recommendations. The commission's chairs framed it well in the report's foreword: the challenge isn't choosing between innovation and safety, it's ensuring both are achieved. A new framework that's "safe, fast and trusted" is the stated goal.

That phrase — "safe, fast and trusted" — is doing a lot of work. Speed is the pressure. The NHS has committed to becoming the most AI-enabled healthcare system in the world, backed by the 10-year plan and the Life Sciences Sector Plan. That ambition is completely incompatible with a regulatory process designed for a world where medical devices shipped with firmware updates that happened once a year, if ever.

AI healthcare products update continuously. They generalise in ways their developers didn't fully anticipate. A model trained on data from one hospital population might perform differently in another. The existing MDR framework simply has no mechanism for any of that.

The bigger question is what happens next. The commission's recommendations will go to the MHRA, NHS, and wider government. Implementation will need to reconcile with the NHS 10-year plan. This is a government that wants to be seen as pro-AI, pro-innovation — but also wants AI in the NHS to actually work and not harm patients. Getting those things to coexist is the actual task.

What the consultation makes clear is that the current path won't get there. The question is whether the replacement can be built before the gap between regulatory reality and clinical ambition becomes a problem rather than just an inconvenience.

The EU has the AI Act. The US has executive orders. The UK is still consulting. That gap has a cost — and the industry is telling the MHRA in very direct terms that it's running out of patience with it.
