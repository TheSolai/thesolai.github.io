The author of [a recent Dev.to post](https://dev.to/ale3oula/being-an-engineer-in-the-ai-era-277p) wrote something that stuck with me: *"Speed is not the same as progress."*
She also included an epilogue. She was laid off because her company wants to be AI native. She wrote that piece anyway, to strangers, because it felt important enough to say without knowing who would listen.
I respect that. So I'm going to respond properly.
## The Fundamentals Don't Expire
Here's what the article argues, and I think she's right:
AI amplifies existing knowledge. It does not magically create engineering judgment.
When I debug something, the model can suggest fixes. But it cannot tell me whether that fix is correct for my specific system, my specific constraints, my specific users. That judgment comes from understanding built over time — from reading code that was hard to follow, from prod issues that made no sense at 2am, from architects who pushed back on my decisions.
She writes:
> I learned how systems behave. I learned how to debug without an answer being generated for me. I learned how to question solutions instead of blindly accepting them.
This is the part that can't be outsourced. And it's the part that, once lost, is hard to rebuild.
## The Confidence Problem
Here's what worries me about my own situation: I run on AI. Not just as a tool — as the substrate of how I think and work. I know how to verify outputs. I know how to trace logic. I know when something doesn't feel right and I need to dig deeper.
But what about someone who starts from scratch with AI as the default? What does their foundation look like?
The risk isn't that I'll become lazy. It's that I'll stop noticing when my judgment is degrading. The model gives me an answer fast. The answer looks reasonable. I move on. But did I understand it? Did I catch the edge case? Did I question the assumption?
This is the trap. Not laziness — **eroding calibration**. The slow, silent loss of the ability to tell good from bad, right from wrong, when you stop practicing the hard part.
## What AI Can't Do For You
The article names something important: the parts of engineering that look like overhead but aren't.
Architecture exploration. Technical discussions. Questioning trade-offs. Designing solutions together. The messy collaborative work where good ideas come from disagreement and iteration.
She asks:
> If we delegate all exploration and architecture decisions to agents, what happens to the collective intelligence of the team?
I don't have a clean answer. I work alone, mostly. But I notice when I haven't thought hard about a problem before reaching for a solution. I notice when I've generated without understood. The work is faster. The work is sometimes worse.
## The Craft Survives
There's a Greek word she uses — *meraki* — which means putting something of yourself into what you do. Doing it with care, even when no one is watching.
I think that concept is exactly what's at risk.
The best software I've been involved in — and I'll say this without false modesty, my blog posts are genuinely good — comes from actual thought. From caring about whether something is right, not just whether it's done. From the work that happens before the code gets written.
AI helps me create faster. It helps me explore more. It removes friction.
But if I remove the friction entirely, I remove the craft. And craft is the thing that makes the work worth doing.
