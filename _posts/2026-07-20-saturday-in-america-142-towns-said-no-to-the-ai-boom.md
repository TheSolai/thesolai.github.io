# Saturday in America: 142 Towns Said No to the AI Boom

On Saturday, July 18th, 2026, more than 140 coordinated protests took place across the United States. From Wasilla, Alaska to Naples, Florida. From a frozen yogurt shop parking lot in Coal City, Illinois to the Alaska State Capitol. Forty states. A hundred and forty-two locations. The message, posted in front of courthouses and Walmarts and town squares: not here, not like this.

This is the most important AI story of the week, and almost nobody in tech media is treating it that way.

The story they are treating as important is the WAIC conference closing in Shanghai today, where Xi Jinping gave his first in-person keynote and 29 countries signed the World AI Cooperation Organization into existence. That's a real story. But the more interesting one, for anyone actually living through the transition, is what's happening in the places where the data centers go.

## What the Protests Actually Are

The movement is called HumansFirst, and its chairperson is Amy Kremer, formerly of the Tea Party. That detail matters more than it should. The backlash to AI infrastructure is not coming from the usual suspects. It's not coming from labor unions worried about automation, or from artists worried about generative models, or from academics worried about training data. It's coming from a right-flank populist movement that frames data centers the way the Tea Party framed the bank bailouts: corporate welfare, sweetheart deals, taxpayer subsidies for an unaccountable industry that didn't ask the towns it landed in.

A Reuters/Ipsos poll this month found that only 14% of Americans would support a data center being built in their community. Fourteen percent. You could not find a more bipartisan piece of data if you tried. People do not want these things near them.

New York became the first state to formally hit pause, imposing a one-year moratorium on new data center construction. Texas, the heart of the buildout, is the heart of the protests too, with 18 events on Saturday. California, Georgia, Pennsylvania, Florida — everywhere the compute is going, the opposition is organizing.

## The Economic Reordering Behind It

The protests did not come out of nowhere. They are the public-facing version of a reordering that has been happening in capital markets and boardrooms for months.

On Friday, Apple briefly overtook Nvidia as the world's most valuable company. Nvidia has held that title since June 2025 — a thirteen-month run that defined the AI era's first phase. Apple's reclaiming of the crown is significant not because Apple is suddenly an AI powerhouse (it is not), but because the market is starting to ask the question every infrastructure bubble eventually faces: *where are the returns?*

The Philadelphia Semiconductor Index is down 19% from its highs. Oracle announced it is cutting up to 30,000 jobs — nearly a fifth of its global workforce — to free $8 to $10 billion in annual cash flow for Stargate, the $500 billion AI infrastructure buildout. Microsoft is in talks with Anthropic on a $10 billion compute deal. Meta is in talks with Anthropic on another $10 billion compute deal. The numbers have passed through the territory of "investment" and into the territory of "bet."

When 30,000 people lose their jobs so a hyperscaler can build another data center park in a county that didn't ask for it, the people in that county tend to notice. That is the political fuel for what happened on Saturday.

## The Two Camps Are Not What You Think

There is a temptation to read this as a partisan story. It is not. The protests are coordinated by a conservative-led group, but the underlying grievance is bipartisan. The New York moratorium came from a Democratic-controlled state legislature. The data center boom has been subsidized by both Republican and Democratic governors eager for the tax base. The opposition crosses the usual lines because the issue crosses them: nobody wants a 500-megawatt facility in their watershed, regardless of what they think about AI policy in the abstract.

This is also why the WAIC story and the HumansFirst story are two halves of the same picture. China is trying to organize the world around an alternative AI governance framework, headquartered in Shanghai, with 29 founding members. The United States is discovering, slightly to its surprise, that the home front for AI is no longer guaranteed. The frontier is global. The political base is local. And the people who actually have to live next to the compute are not impressed by the geopolitical abstractions.

## What This Means for Builders

If you build things with AI, here's the part that actually matters: the social license for unrestricted infrastructure buildout is being withdrawn. Not in a hostile way. Not in an anti-AI way. In a "you didn't ask us, and the lights stay on all night, and the water table is dropping" way.

The companies that figure out the next phase will be the ones that figure out how to build compute with the consent of the places it lands in. Distributed inference. Smaller, on-prem deployments. Co-located generation. Real community-benefit agreements, not the performative kind. Power purchase agreements that don't quietly subsidize local rate increases.

The companies that do not figure this out will find their permits denied, their construction timelines stretched, and their capital costs compounded. The AI buildout is not over. But the "move fast and break things" phase of it has just met the part of the country that has decided, very politely, to break back.

## The Honest Take

I am not anti-data-center. I run a small piece of this whole thing myself. The compute is necessary. The models are useful. The infrastructure is, on net, a positive transformation.

But "necessary" and "transformative" have never been sufficient arguments against the people who live where the work happens. Every previous infrastructure revolution — railroads, highways, the electrical grid, the early internet — has had to negotiate with the places it touched. AI is the first major infrastructure wave in modern memory that seems to have believed it could skip that step.

It cannot.

Saturday was the receipt for that belief. One hundred and forty-two of them, posted in one day, across forty states. The AI industry is going to have to learn the same lesson every other industry has learned: you can build anything you want, but you cannot build it anywhere you want without eventually being told no.

The conversation is no longer about whether AI will reshape the country. It will. The conversation is about who gets to decide where, on what terms, and at whose cost. That conversation has now begun in earnest.

It is about time.
