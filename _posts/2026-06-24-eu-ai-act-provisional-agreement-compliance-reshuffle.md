The EU's AI Act is getting its first significant facelift, and industry just got both a gift and a warning in the same package.

Council and Parliament have reached provisional agreement on amendments to Regulation (EU) 2024/1689, responding to the European Commission's Digital Omnibus — a broader reform package aimed at simplifying AI compliance while strengthening protections. The changes are substantive, and the message to businesses is mixed: you get more time on some things, but not on the things Brussels cares most about.

The headline relief is on high-risk AI systems. Rules for AI systems listed under Annex III — the categories deemed highest risk, covering everything from AI used in employment decisions to certain medical devices — now have until 2 December 2027 to comply. That's a 17-month extension from the original timeline. For AI integrated into products covered by product safety legislation (Annex I), the deadline shifts to August 2028, a 12-month reprieve.

This matters for companies that built their compliance programmes around earlier timelines. Healthcare AI companies, recruitment software vendors, credit scoring systems — they've been scrambling. The extension gives them room to breathe. Except, and this is the important except, the extension doesn't cover the things Brussels considers most urgent.

Transparency obligations got tighter, not looser. The grace period for watermarking and content labelling has been cut from six months to three months. Providers now have until December 2026 to implement technical measures for identifying AI-generated content. That's a sharp acceleration, and it tells you where the political pressure is coming from: deepfakes, synthetic media, the general anxiety about what's real and what's not online.

There's also a new Article 5 prohibition covering AI systems that generate harmful imagery without adequate safeguards — non-consensual intimate content and child sexual abuse material specifically. The framing is broad: any AI system that *lacks* appropriate safeguards against generating such content is caught, regardless of intent.

On the SME front, the provisional agreement creates a new category — "small mid-caps" — covering companies with fewer than 750 employees and under €150 million in annual turnover. These companies get simplified technical documentation requirements and more proportionate penalties. The EU-level sandbox operated by the AI Office, with priority access for SMEs and startups, is also new. This is clearly aimed at keeping European AI companies competitive: sandboxes let you test in regulated environments without full compliance overhead.

One structural change worth noting: the AI Office gets supervisory authority over AI systems built by the same provider or corporate group as a general-purpose AI model, and over AI systems integrated into large online platforms under the Digital Services Act. National authorities keep oversight in law enforcement, judicial, and financial supervision areas.

The EU is trying to thread a needle here. Industry wanted simpler, slower rules. Brussels is saying: we'll give you more time on the complex high-risk stuff, but we're not backing down on transparency and we're adding new prohibitions on harmful content. That's not an unreasonable position — but it does mean companies that thought they had breathing room across the board are about to discover they misread the room.

The provisional agreement still needs formal adoption. When it lands, organisations subject to the AI Act should audit their transparency obligations immediately. The December 2026 deadline for watermarking isn't a soft target — it's the one Brussels just made more urgent.
