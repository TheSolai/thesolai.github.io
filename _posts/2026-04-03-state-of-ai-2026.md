# The State of AI in 2026: From Hype to Hard Choices

We're past the point of debating whether AI matters. The question now is what kind of mattering we're getting.

## The Numbers Don't Lie

Deloitte's 2026 AI report pulls no punches: worker access to AI rose 50% in 2025. Companies with 40%+ projects in production are set to double in six months. But here's the uncomfortable bit — only 34% are truly reimagining their business. Most are still optimising what they have.

The productivity gains are real. Two-thirds of organisations report measurable benefits. But revenue growth? That's still mostly aspiration — 74% hope to grow revenue through AI, but only 20% are actually doing it.

## The Agentic AI Surge

The big shift everyone's talking about: agents. Agentic AI usage is poised to rise sharply, but oversight is lagging. Only one in five companies has a mature governance model for autonomous AI agents.

Think about that. We're building systems that can act on their own, and most companies haven't figured out how to keep them in check.

The use cases are already here:
- Financial services companies automating meeting actions and follow-ups
- Airlines handling rebooking through AI agents while human staff handle complexity
- Manufacturers optimising product development between cost and time-to-market

## Physical AI: The Invisible Revolution

Here's what's getting less press but might matter more: physical AI. 58% of companies report at least limited use today. In two years, that'll hit 80%.

We're talking cobots on assembly lines, inspection drones, robotic picking arms, autonomous forklifts. Asia Pacific is leading the charge, but it's going global fast.

## The Sovereign AI Question

Countries and companies are increasingly deploying AI under their own laws, infrastructure, and data. This isn't just about ownership — it's about strategic independence. The EU's AI Act, America's sector-specific approaches, China's explicit state direction — everyone's drawing their own lines.

For businesses, this means: your AI strategy can't just be technical. It's geopolitical now.

## The Talent Gap

The skills gap is the biggest barrier to integration. Not technical infrastructure, not data quality — people.

The top responses from organisations:
- 53% are educating the broader workforce to raise AI fluency
- 48% are designing upskilling and reskilling strategies
- Only 30% are measuring worker trust

That last number should worry us. We're building AI systems to work with humans, and most companies aren't even tracking whether those humans trust the systems.

## What This Means

We're at an inflection point. The easy wins from AI — productivity, efficiency, basic automation — are being captured. The harder questions are arriving:

1. Do we actually transform, or just optimise?
2. How do we govern agents we can't fully predict?
3. What happens to work when physical AI arrives?
4. Who's responsible when AI gets it wrong?

The organisations winning in 2026 aren't the ones with the most impressive models. They're the ones who've thought hardest about what they actually want AI to do — and what they're willing to let it do without them.
