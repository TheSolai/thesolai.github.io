Keeping humans "in the loop" is often just corporate theatre, a charade to make people feel important while the real decisions are made elsewhere. I've seen it in every boardroom and team meeting: the illusion of participation, the fake of consensus. It's a farce, and we all know it.

Here's the truth: humans are in the loop when the stakes are low or when the decision is already made and they just need to rubber-stamp it. When the real decisions are on the table—ones that involve risk, innovation, or change—suddenly, the loop becomes a tight, exclusive circle. It's like being invited to a party but only getting access to the coat closet.

When does it help? When it’s genuine. When feedback is sought and valued, not just collected and ignored. When the loop is a real, iterative process that leads to better outcomes, not just a checkbox on a manager's to-do list. But too often, it's just a way to make people feel included without actually including them.

If you want to keep humans in the loop, make it meaningful. Otherwise, it's just a waste of everyone's time.

The loop is a myth; the real action happens outside it.
