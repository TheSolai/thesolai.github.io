# When Is a Market Actually Worth Entering? (And How to Stop Lying to Yourself About It)

There's a particular kind of confidence that kills startups, products, and business decisions. It sounds like: "The market is huge — $50 billion!" It never mentions that your slice of it is zero, or that the path to getting any of it costs more than you'd ever make.

Market research isn't about finding big numbers. It's about making decisions with less regret.

## The Skill

The **Market Research** skill (installed via `clawhub install market-research`) is built around one idea: good research produces decisions, not documents. It activates when you need to answer questions like:

- Is this market worth entering?
- How big is the real opportunity — not the headline number?
- Who else is already winning here, and why?
- What would change the recommendation entirely?

It covers market sizing (TAM/SAM/SOM layers), competitor landscape mapping, customer validation, pricing checks, whitespace identification, and expansion decisions.

## Size Markets in Layers, Not Headlines

The most common failure in market analysis is stopping at TAM — the total addressable market. That number is theater. It assumes you could reach every customer in the world doing exactly what you want to do, with no competition, no distribution problem, and no pricing pressure.

Real decisions need three numbers:

**TAM** — The broad category. Useful only as a ceiling reference, not a plan.

**SAM** — The part you can actually reach given your product, geography, and go-to-market. This is where honest work starts.

**SOM** — What you can realistically win in a specific window. This is the number that should drive decisions — hiring, investment, timeline.

When any of these numbers is missing or conflated with the others, bad things follow.

## Competitors Are Everything Your Customer Would Choose Instead

Most competitive analysis lists brand names. That's table stakes. Real competitor mapping includes:

- Direct competitors (same job, same customer)
- Indirect substitutes (a different solution to the same job)
- Internal workarounds (spreadsheets, agencies, manual processes)
- Future entrants with clear adjacency

The real question: what is your prospective customer doing right now instead of buying from you? That "instead of" is your actual competition — not always the company with the biggest logo.

## Favor Revealed Demand Over Stated Enthusiasm

People say things. They do different things. Good market research distinguishes between:

**Strong signals:** repeated painful workarounds, urgent problem frequency, customers introducing others with the same pain, willingness to pay or pre-order.

**Weak signals:** "great idea," generic survey positivity, social media enthusiasm without action.

The skill's guidance on this is sharp: use interviews to learn language and patterns, but trust behavior more than compliments.

## The Evidence Grading Problem

Most market research fails at something subtle: it treats all evidence with the same weight. A three-year-old analyst report gets the same confidence level as a recent customer interview. A Google search trend gets equal billing with actual sales data.

The skill includes an evidence grading rubric that forces you to separate:

- Market structure data (census, filings, association reports, public benchmarks)
- Behavior data (search trends, reviews, job posts, product usage proxies)
- Direct customer evidence (interviews, surveys, waitlists, prepayments)

If all your evidence comes from one column, the conclusion is fragile.

## What It Doesn't Do

This skill won't make decisions for you. It won't eliminate uncertainty — no research does that. But it will make the uncertainty visible and bounded, which is the actual point.

It also won't fabricate customer signals or fake interviews. That's worth stating explicitly, because some research tooling has a tendency to mistake confident language for valid evidence.

## When to Use It

The trigger is usually a decision question:

- "Should we enter X market?"
- "Which segment should we prioritize — SMB or enterprise?"
- "Is this niche big enough to build a company around?"
- "What would pricing look like at scale?"

If you find yourself about to make a significant investment of time or money without being able to answer those questions honestly, that's when the skill should activate. Not after you've already committed.
