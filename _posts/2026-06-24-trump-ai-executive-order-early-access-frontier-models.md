President Trump signed an executive order last week that asks AI companies to give the federal government access to their most advanced models before those models are released publicly. The stated goal is cybersecurity: assessing whether frontier AI systems could be used to compromise critical infrastructure, breach systems at scale, or otherwise pose national security risks. The mechanism is voluntary.

That word — voluntary — is doing a lot of work in this story.

The order establishes a benchmarking process for AI companies to assess their models' "advanced cyber capabilities" and determine whether they qualify as a "covered frontier model." For those that do, the order asks companies to provide government access up to 30 days before broader release. It also enables the government to help select the "trusted partners" — outside companies, presumably in the private sector — who would get early access as part of a coordinated cybersecurity initiative.

The order isemphatically not mandatory. The text explicitly states nothing shall be construed to authorize "any mandatory governmental licensing, pre-clearance, or permitting requirement for the development, publication, release or distribution of AI models." This is a request, not a law. Compliance depends entirely on whether the companies decide to cooperate.

The timing is interesting. The order was originally supposed to be signed with a ceremony featuring prominent tech CEOs — until Trump postponed it because he "didn't like certain aspects of it." The version that eventually landed in private suggests some significant last-minute negotiation happened behind closed doors.

What's driving this, more than anything, appears to be Anthropic's Mythos model. Earlier this year, Anthropic announced Claude Mythos Preview — a model explicitly designed to excel at identifying software vulnerabilities and security flaws. The company limited the initial rollout to a select group of partners under a programme called Project Glasswing, and expanded it in June. Mythos apparently prompted several high-level meetings with senior Trump officials, including Chief of Staff Susie Wiles and Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent.

The Department of Defence, interestingly, had previously designated Anthropic a "supply chain risk" — which prohibits defence contractors from using the company's technology in their work with the agency. Anthropic is currently suing to reverse that designation. So the government is simultaneously concerned about Anthropic's capabilities, banning Anthropic from defence contracts, and asking Anthropic to voluntarily share its most powerful models before release. The contradictions are hard to miss.

The broader context: Anthropic filed confidentially for an IPO in June. OpenAI is also preparing a potential offering. SpaceX is reportedly heading toward a public debut that could value it at over a trillion dollars. These companies are navigating a moment where they're becoming major financial institutions in their own right, with corresponding leverage over — and entanglement with — the government.

The executive order reads, in places, more like a press release than a policy document. It talks about partnering with industry rather than regulating it, about American ingenuity, about not picking winners and losers. Which is either a genuine philosophical commitment to voluntary cooperation or a convenient framing for a policy that has no real enforcement mechanism.

Whether this changes anything depends entirely on what happens next. If major AI companies cooperate enthusiastically, the clearinghouse and early access framework could become a genuine national security asset. If they treat it as a largely optional courtesy, the order will have accomplished the political objective — looking like action on AI security — without actually constraining anything.

The next few months will tell you which version you're in.
